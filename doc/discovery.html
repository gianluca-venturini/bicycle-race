Discovery

What we discovered using this tool

General findings

In this section we focus on the first, most evident findings we were able to discover playing with the application.


  • One of the first "weird" behaviors we noticed when looking at the "sum of trips per day of the year" chart is that there's a huge low peak around mid september: this is the most evident outlier of the chart.
    Going into more detail, on September 15th there's indeed a big drop in the number of rides, which go from an average number of 6000 to 1500 on this day.
    First we looked for particular events on this day but there's nothing so relevant to justify these data. A deeper analysis, however, can explain this phenomenon: if we look, through the day control and the slider, at the temperatures of this day wrt to the previous days of september, we can notice that this is the first day in which the temperatures drop from a previous average above 70° to an average of 60° exactly on Sept. 15.
    For this reason a likely explanation of this pattern can be that people are less willing to use the bike when a big climate drop occurs, showing how big a factor the weather can play in this scenario.

  • Let us consider the highest peak in the "sum of trips per day of the year" chart: this happens to be on September 28th, with a number of bikes which is over 10'000.
    Analyzing the data, we can see that, as happened before, the weather conditions and the temperature are fundmental: in this day we experience an average temperature of 80° which is higher than the trend for this period, so people are more willing to go out and use bikes. Moreover, if we look at the "bikes out per day of the week" chart we notice that this is a Saturday, so even more people should have the possibility and the time to ride a bike on this day.
  • In general, the points of the chart form a trend which makes sense: the slope is lower in the summer, with people mostly out of town or less willing to use the bike in hot days, then it increases during the Fall months and finally decreases again in the winter as the temperature and the weather conditions worsen.

Bikes out per day of the week

Trips per day of the year


The above charts reflect that the usage of these stations belongs mainly to college students: there's a significant decrease of bikes out during the weekend with respect to weekdays.
Moreover, considering the year (or the few months available from our data), we can notice that the usage is low in the summer months, while it begins to increase in the second half of august, i.e. when the classes start. It then remains constant until december, when the visible decrease can be due both to the winter temperatures and/or holidays.

Now let's consider the type of user of these stations: as easily expected, if we look at the pie chart for the user type of the UIC stations and compare it to that of 2 touristic stations such as Millennium Park and Navy Pier, we can see that while in the university area the vast majority is represented by "subscribers", i.e. students with a lomg term subscription, in the mostly touristic stations the majority are customers, i.e. people who need only an occasional use of the bikes.

The station in the street where the event takes place is added to compareAll (highlighted in red).

Weather patterns

In this section we focus on the patterns related to weather conditions.

When you need to run fast

In the following maps we can see a trip (the longest one), done in two different consecutive days of July, at the same hour.
While on the 19th the weather is nice and the person takes around 40 minutes to complete the trip between the two stations, on the 18th there's a thunderstorm and the same trip (same person?) is completed in around 20 minutes. This let us say that the weather condition influenced the speed of the riders.

Weather, temperature and behaviors

One of the most interesting correlations to investigate is that between the behavior of riders according to weather conditions and temperature. Weather and temperature have the same impact on a rider's decision to use a bike? Are they correlated or are there more factors to be taken into account?

The maps below show the same situations of the previous example, extended to the selection of all the stations. We can see that the overall behavior shown both by the rides on the map and on the charts is not so different between the two situations even if the first one has good weather conditions while the second has thunderstorms. We can also notice that the temperature here, given it is July, is around 90° in both cases. We cannot say that given a high temperature, even if there's a thunderstorm, people have the same behavior as with clear weather, but we can say that even if there's bad weather, given that the temperature is good and we are in a summer month where the storms can possibly go away quickly, most people are not so likely to renounce to their rides.


Now let's consider different weather in two consecutive fall/winter Sundays. In the maps below we can see that while on Nov 10th, with sunny weather, the number of rides reaches a peak of 500 and decreases only at the end of the day, on Nov 17th the presence of a heavy thunderstorm, and in general of bad weather conditions all along the day, brings to a widely reduced bike usage with a peak of 200 and then a decreasing usage as the bad weather conditions remain stable, even if here the temperature is higher.

Sunrise/sunset patterns

In this section we focus on looking for relations between rides and sunrise and sunset time.

After a close look at many examples, we can say that there's a clear relation between the imcrease in the number of rides in the morning correspondent to the sunrise time: the "bikes out per hour of day" chart clearly shows this.
This pattern is more complicated to be stated for the sunset: even if many times the sunset coincides the the point of the chart in which the number of bikes decreases, sometimes the bad weather conditions or other factors influence the chart.
